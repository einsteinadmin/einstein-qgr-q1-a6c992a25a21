What a C → B → B progression actually means

Three lenses, three different questions, three independent answers:

  • Lens 1 (C) asks "did we hit our plan?" — honest answer: no, the NOI target was 13.63% margin and we delivered 8.31%. Revenue got to 93.7% of target. Most other metrics hit. C is a fair absolute grade — not great, not catastrophic.
  • Lens 2 (B) asks "did we outperform the market?" — Texas housing was roughly flat; Einstein revenue grew +6.9% YoY. ~8 pt outperformance vs peers who are treading water. B per rubric.
  • Lens 3 (B) asks "did we improve on ourselves?" — Revenue up 6.9%, NOI up 37.5%, margin up 1.8 pts. Clear "significant metric improvement" on the rubric. B.

The progression pattern matters more than any individual letter. C → B → B says: "we set an ambitious plan and missed it, but we outgrew the market and outperformed last year." Planning-calibration story, not an execution-failure story.

Compare to Q4'25's progression (B / A- / B+): we were ON plan then, and the big outperformance was market-adjusted (Texas housing had declined more sharply). Different story shape, different strategic response.

What went right (the two B's): Underlying business grew, pricing held, margin expanded, NPS stayed at 91.9. Every structural health indicator moved the right direction. The market gave us a ~flat tailwind and we grew meaningfully above it.

What went wrong (the C): February. The 13.63% NOI plan assumed costs would flex with seasonal revenue — they mostly don't. February ran a -2.3% NOI margin because fixed costs don't shrink in slow season. March and April recovered to 10.9% and 13.5%. Q1 is structurally back-loaded; the plan didn't account for that.

Three threads worth pulling at the Ownership QGR:

  1. Q2'26 target calibration: If Q2 follows historical pattern, margins recover naturally (summer season). Don't "set harder Q2 targets to make up for Q1" — that's a planning error. Q1 is Q1, Q2 is Q2.
  2. Annual target math: Full-year NOI is still trackable to plan if Q2-Q4 execute. Worth modeling whether the overall target needs adjustment given Q1 data.
  3. Plan calibration for FY'27 (next fiscal year): The lesson isn't "execute harder" — it's "plan assumptions for February are wrong." Whoever sets FY'27 targets should explicitly bake in a February loss-month model rather than assuming linear margin flex across the quarter.

⚠️ Methodology Change Callout — New in v3.1 Reporting

Two compounding methodology changes affect Q1'26 conversion rate comparability with prior periods.

1. April 5, 2026 autobooking form launch (mid-quarter CRM funnel change). Prior to April 5, users who got an "inestimable" quote result had to fill out a second form to be routed to sales. Many dropped off. After April 5, contact info is captured BEFORE the inestimable gate — those users now route directly to sales. Net effect: lead volume roughly doubled, with lower aggregate intent.

What a 6.3% revenue miss actually means

A 6.3% miss sounds small, and in absolute dollars it is ($366K). The more useful framing: revenue was up +6.9% YoY but came in ~6% short of a plan that expected higher growth. Einstein grew — just not as fast as the plan assumed. This is a planning-calibration story as much as an execution one.

One caveat on how to read the CRM comparison table: Matisen's Mongo pull shows $1.73M in future-scheduled Q1'26 bookings (980 moves with service dates beyond Apr 18). Q1'25 shows $0 in the equivalent field, but only because those bookings have since resolved (serviced or cancelled) — not because Q1'25 had no pipeline at its equivalent point in time. Treat the $1.73M as "visible Q2 service backlog we can track," not as "more pipeline than we had last year."

Two forces pulling in opposite directions:

  • Pulling down: Conversion rate dropped 10.6 pts YoY on the canonical formula. Some methodological (Apr 5 autobooking form), some real (pricing ceilings at specific branches).
  • Ticket growth partially offsetting: Avg booked ticket grew +12.8% YoY ($1,332 → $1,502). Every lead that converts is worth more than a year ago.

Three distinct levers for Q2'26:

  1. Recover conversion on existing lead flow — better triage on Apr 5 cohort moves blended rate back toward 35-42% historical range.
  2. Set Q2'26 revenue targets using verified Q1'26 metrics — revenue +6.9% YoY, NOI +37.5%, ticket +12.8%. Don't bake $1.73M future-bookings into Q2 as "already-won."
  3. Decide whether the 93% lead volume jump is durable — if yes, build sales capacity. If partly one-time integration effect, don't over-build.

The NOI story is cost structure, not volume. Even if revenue had hit target, Einstein would have missed NOI by ~$290K due to expense structure running above plan. February problem (-$34K company NOI in Feb alone) compounded by below-plan branch execution in CTXH.

What "expense structure running above plan" actually means

The plan assumed costs at 86.37% of revenue (the inverse of the 13.63% target NOI margin). What actually happened was costs at 91.69% of revenue — 5.32 percentage points higher than plan.

Only 15% of the NOI miss came from the revenue shortfall. 85% came from the cost structure being thicker than planned.

Why — the February fixed-cost problem: Think of a restaurant with $10K/month rent. Plan for 1,000 customers, get 900 — per-customer cost goes up because that $10K gets spread over fewer people. The restaurant didn't actually spend more; the costs just ate a bigger share of thinner revenue.

That's what happened to Einstein in February. Full-quarter fixed costs (trucks, insurance, salaried ops team, facilities) against thin winter revenue = -2.3% margin. March recovered to 10.9% and April to 13.5% (near-target). The 5.32 pt margin miss is concentrated in February, not spread evenly.

Three planning options for FY'27:

  1. February revenue grows — marketing push, pricing strategy, or non-moving revenue pilot specifically for slow season.
  2. February costs become more variable — more contractor labor, fewer salaried fixed roles, facility/fleet right-sizing.
  3. Future quarterly targets adjust for February structurally — budget Feb as a cash-burn month, not a missed month.

What a -10.6 pt conversion drop actually means

Conversion rate is the share of every dollar we quote that turns into a booked or serviced dollar. Q1'26 came in at 33.2%, down 10.6 pts from Q1'25's 43.8%.

The instinct when conversion drops is to read it as "customers are saying no more often." Partly true, but the bigger story in Q1'26 is the denominator changing character, not the customers. Same top funnel, new gate behavior. The April 5 form captures contact info BEFORE a user hits an "inestimable" dead-end — so people who would have dropped off last year now count as leads this year. Lower aggregate intent, same absolute volume of good bookings. Conversion arithmetic goes down even when booking behavior hasn't really changed.

Avg Booked Ticket up +12.8% YoY ($1,332 → $1,502) is the cleanest signal in this table. Every dollar that does convert is a bigger dollar. Pricing working, alongside the "conversion decline."

Three forces, compounding:

  • Methodology: Post-Apr 5 autobooking form surfaces leads that previously evaporated. Cohort B 27.4% vs Cohort A 35.5%.
  • Pricing effects at specific branches: North Austin elasticity at -1.03 (at ceiling on revenue, likely margin-favorable), Leander -24 pt drop compounded with confirmed availability constraints (72% personnel-budget avg).
  • Matched window change: Matisen's re-pull uses 77-day matched windows. The prior draft compared different-length windows, artificially understating the YoY magnitude.

Why the autobooking form changed what counts as a lead

Before April 5: user → quote flow → if inestimable, hits a wall, must submit second form → most drop off → CRM records "lead" only for users who completed the second form.

After April 5: user → contact info captured upfront → quote flow → if inestimable, gets an auto-reach-out from sales → CRM records "lead" for everyone who got that far. More leads captured, but many are "tire-kickers" who would have left before.

The conversion rate drop from 35.5% to 27.4% is exactly what you'd predict when you widen the lead definition — same-ish numerator (real bookings), bigger denominator (old leads + newly-captured hesitators). The new baseline isn't 35%, it's whatever comes out of the post-Apr 5 system with proper triage.

Three decisions worth making explicit:

  1. Triage Cohort B differently. Don't measure reps against a 35% target when handling 27%-baseline leads.
  2. Maximize directBook subset value. 86% conversion on 675 leads/14 days. Losing a directBook lead to unavailability costs more than losing a Cohort B lead.
  3. Set a realistic Cohort B floor. The question isn't "can we get Cohort B to 35%?" It's "what's the floor below which Cohort B becomes unprofitable to serve?"

What an elasticity of -1.03 actually means — and why margin framing matters

Elasticity measures how much customers flinch when price goes up. An elasticity of -1 is the "ceiling" — every 1% price increase costs exactly 1 point of conversion. Above -1 (closer to zero), customers don't flinch much — room to raise. Below -1 (more negative), pricing past what the market will bear.

North Austin's -1.03 puts it exactly at the pain point. +8.3% price → -8.6 pt conversion. Dollar-for-dollar trade. Raising further loses money; holding here is at the edge.

"Over ceiling" — but only on the revenue framing. -1.03 means revenue is roughly flat. But the margin frame is more favorable:

  • At 8.3% higher ticket, variable costs don't scale 1-for-1 with price (labor + truck time are similar regardless of quoted rate)
  • Fewer-but-higher-margin moves can produce HIGHER gross margin even when revenue is flat
  • North Austin's 15.4% NOI margin suggests the rate increase is likely margin-accretive

The decision depends on which metric we optimize for. Revenue framing → A/B rollback test makes sense. NOI/cash framing → holding is defensible, maybe good.

Einstein's $2,000 damage coverage advantage (included with no deductible) vs competitors' standard coverage (worthless $0.60/lb or extra $100-300 for full coverage) is NOT being articulated during the sales process.

At a $10-15/hr premium over competitors offering $0 coverage, the differential is economically justified when all-in cost is calculated. This is a value communication issue, not a pricing issue.
